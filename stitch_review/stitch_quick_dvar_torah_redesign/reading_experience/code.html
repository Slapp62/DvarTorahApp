The Power of Speech: Echoes of the Soul

Rabbi Jonathan Miller

Senior Lecturer at The Eternal Archive

In the tapestry of human existence, few threads are as potent as the words we weave. The Torah begins not with an action, but with a declaration: "And G-d said." Creation itself is a linguistic event. This teaches us a fundamental truth about our own nature—our speech is not merely a tool for communication, but a mechanism of creation and destruction.

"Words are the wings of the soul; where they fly, the spirit follows."
— Ancient Commentary

Consider the concept of Lashon Hara. Often translated simply as "evil speech," its implications run much deeper. When we speak ill of another, we aren't just conveying information; we are altering the spiritual reality of that person in the world. We are, in a sense, unmaking the divine image they carry.

Conversely, the power of a kind word—a Milah Tovah—is equally transformative. A single sentence of genuine encouragement can act as a catalyst for a person's entire trajectory. It is the spiritual equivalent of watering a parched seedling.

As we navigate our modern digital landscape, where words are often cheapened by their sheer volume and speed, we must return to this scholarly mindfulness. Every text, every comment, and every conversation is an act of creation. The question we must ask ourselves is: What kind of world are we building with our breath today?

Points for Reflection

  • 01. Recall a time a single word changed your perspective on a difficult day.
  • 02. How can we practice 'digital silence' to preserve the sanctity of our speech?
